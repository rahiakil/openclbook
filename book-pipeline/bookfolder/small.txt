The Lighthouse at Grey Cove

Mara found the door unlocked—again—and the lamp in the tower burning low, as if someone had just left. She climbed the spiral with salt in her pocket and a promise on her tongue: tonight she would learn who relit the wick.

At the lantern room, footprints crossed the dust in arcs, too small for boots, too deliberate for gulls. The lens caught the moon and threw it seaward in a blade of silver. Out past the reef, a second light answered, once, twice, then dark.

“Not a ship,” Mara whispered. The tide was wrong for flares.

She trimmed the wick anyway. The beam steadied. The answering pulse did not return—but the footprints led down, not up, vanishing where the stone met air, as though the keeper had stepped off the world and taken the story with her.

Mara locked the door behind her. Some invitations, she decided, were meant to expire unseen.
